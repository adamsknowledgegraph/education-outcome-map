# Lifestyle Model

## Goal

Show students what a career path feels like in practice, not just what it pays.

This should make tradeoffs explicit:

- money
- working hours
- schedule quality
- free time
- predictability

## MVP Principle

Keep it simple.

Use real data where available.
When a dimension is not available in a dataset, use reasonable heuristic assumptions and label them as estimates.

## Core Lifestyle Dimensions

### 1. Earnings Power

What to show:

- annual pay
- hourly pay

Why it matters:

- annual salary shows headline earnings
- hourly pay makes the lived reality easier to understand

### 2. Average Weekly Hours

What to show:

- estimated average weekly hours

Examples:

- 35-40 hours
- 40-50 hours
- 50-60 hours
- 60+ hours

Why it matters:

- this reveals how much time the money actually costs

### 3. Schedule Type

What to show:

- normal hours
- extended hours
- shift work
- irregular / on-call

Definitions:

- `normal hours`: mostly daytime, weekdays, weekends usually free
- `extended hours`: still mostly daytime, but long days are common
- `shift work`: nights, early mornings, rotating shifts, weekends common
- `irregular / on-call`: timing can change unpredictably

Why it matters:

- two jobs with similar pay can produce completely different lives

### 4. Time Boundary Quality

What to show:

- how likely work is to spill into evenings, weekends, or personal time

Simple levels:

- strong boundaries
- moderate boundaries
- weak boundaries

Examples:

- tile-laying: often strong boundaries
- investment banking: often weak boundaries

### 5. Lifestyle Fit

What to show:

- whether the path realistically supports a chosen lifestyle tier in a chosen cost tier

Lifestyle tiers:

- simple
- comfortable
- premium

This should not mean "luxury aesthetic."
It should mean what life is realistically supportable.

### 6. Predictability

What to show:

- low
- medium
- high

Meaning:

- how predictable the hours and routine are

Why it matters:

- predictable lives feel very different from high-variability ones even at the same income

### 7. AI Exposure

What to show:

- low
- medium
- high

Meaning:

- how likely the role is to be automated, compressed, or materially changed by AI

Why it matters:

- students should know whether a path is durable or likely to be disrupted

Examples:

- plumber: often low
- nurse: often low to medium
- consultant: often medium to high
- some analyst / knowledge-work roles: often high

### 8. Geographic Concentration

What to show:

- local-anywhere
- regional
- major-hub-dependent

Meaning:

- how much the career depends on being in a major city or specific professional hub

Why it matters:

- some paths can be pursued almost anywhere
- others effectively require moving to a major metro or industry center

Examples:

- electrician: often local-anywhere
- teacher: often local-anywhere
- investment banking: often major-hub-dependent
- big law / elite consulting: often major-hub-dependent

## Lifestyle Tiers

### Simple

Profile:

- values stability and lower spending
- fewer luxury expectations
- affordability and free time matter more than status

Implied budget style:

- modest housing
- careful discretionary spending
- local leisure
- limited premium travel/luxury purchases

### Comfortable

Profile:

- wants reasonable comfort and convenience
- can spend on hobbies, travel, and quality-of-life improvements
- still cares about balance

Implied budget style:

- decent housing
- regular dining/socializing
- some travel
- room to save

### Premium

Profile:

- wants high spending power and access to expensive preferences
- premium housing, experiences, taste, flexibility, or status matter a lot

Implied budget style:

- expensive city viability
- premium consumption
- higher expectations on discretionary spend

Important:

The dashboard should make clear that premium lifestyles often come with higher time costs and worse schedule quality.

## Compromise Dimensions

This is the heart of the product.

Users should clearly see what they are trading.

### Main tradeoffs to expose

- more money vs less free time
- more prestige vs more stress and unpredictability
- better schedule vs lower pay ceiling
- stronger boundaries vs slower earnings growth
- high earnings vs higher AI exposure
- prestige / pay vs needing to live in a major hub

## Example Career Signals

These are heuristic examples for MVP thinking:

- investment banking:
  - high pay
  - high weekly hours
  - weak boundaries
  - low predictability
  - high AI exposure pressure on parts of the workflow
  - major-hub-dependent
  - premium lifestyle support, but large time sacrifice

- nursing:
  - solid pay
  - shift work likely
  - weekends/nights possible
  - lower AI exposure than many desk roles
  - local-anywhere
  - decent earnings, but schedule compromises

- tile-laying:
  - moderate pay
  - often daytime schedule
  - stronger boundaries
  - low AI exposure
  - local-anywhere
  - physically demanding, but clearer end-of-day boundaries

- software engineering:
  - moderate to high pay
  - often normal or extended hours
  - medium to strong boundaries depending on employer
  - medium AI exposure
  - regional to major-hub-dependent depending on role
  - good balance potential

## What The User Should Feel

After exploring a path, the user should be able to say:

- "This pays well, but I would lose evenings."
- "This path supports a comfortable life, but not my premium taste."
- "This has lower pay, but I keep my weekends."
- "This is a better match for the life I actually want."
